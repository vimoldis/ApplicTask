Application Task · Reimagine a Feature for Love

From shaping who you are
to anchoring who you truly are

A critical response to the TrueYou Visualizer feature, and a reimagined alternative built on authenticity, human growth, and digital co-existence.

Task 1A · Identify what feels "off"

Two core weaknesses in TrueYou Visualizer

  • It erases the real person. By automatically adjusting facial features, skin tone and micro-expressions to match "regional attractiveness trends", the feature doesn't enhance but replaces. Individuality, charisma, and authentic presence are quietly removed. Users end up looking slightly the same, which defeats the very purpose of connection.
  • It creates a gap between expectation and reality. When the enhanced profile image doesn't match the real person in a date, it breeds confusion, insecurity and disappointment, all the opposite of confidence. For people already struggling with self-esteem, OCD, autism, perfectionism, or body image, this gap can actively deepen psychological harm rather than support healthy relationships.
Task 1B · Consider the wider impact

Who gets hurt and why it matters

The AI-generated concept overlooks several groups: people with neurodiverse conditions who rely on predictability and truth; LGBTQ+ individuals and those who already challenge social beauty norms; and anyone navigating a society that is still learning to accept difference.

By optimising for "regional attractiveness trends", the feature reinforces existing social hierarchies and beauty stereotypes rather than dismantling them. Technology that shapes who people appear to be rather than helping them be seen as they are that doesn't serve humanity. It serves an algorithm.

The broader risk is cultural: when every profile is optimised toward the same ideal, diversity disappears. And with it, the very thing that makes human connection meaningful.

Task 2 · A more human alternative

Introducing SelfAnchor

The Concept
Stay rooted in yourself.
Then find someone real.

SelfAnchor is a connection platform built on one core belief: you cannot build a real relationship if you don't know who you are first. Instead of optimising your image for algorithms, SelfAnchor helps you grow — at your own pace, on your own terms, and then connect with others from a place of genuine confidence.

Not for everyone — intentionally designed for those who need it most.

Task 3 · Explain your thinking

Reasoning trace

How I interpreted the original concept
TrueYou Visualizer presents itself as a confidence tool, but functions as an erasure tool. It solves a surface problem like insecurity about appearance, way of communication, behavior, environment, choices and decisions, while deepening the root cause: disconnection from one's real self.
Key decisions I made
I chose to flip the entire premise and instead of changing how you look to others, help you become more grounded in who you are. The name SelfAnchor reflects this as stability before connection.
One assumption or limitation I chose
SelfAnchor is not for everyone. It is intentionally designed for those who need it most. This limitation is a feature, not a weakness. A tool that tries to serve everyone often serves no one well.
How it supports positive human outcomes
By building confidence and social skills before connection, SelfAnchor prepares people for real relationships, not optimised ones. Even if a match doesn't last, the person grows because experience is the result of mistakes we have learned from. The app's goal is to make itself unnecessary.
What I would explore next
How to design the growth levels inclusively — particularly for neurodiverse users, LGBTQ+ communities, people recovering from digital dependency, with different level sickness and trauma, autistic people, with OCD or low self-worth.
The core difference in one sentence
TrueYou Visualizer changes who you appear to be from self-dependent to app-dependent, while SelfAnchor helps you become more fully who you already are.
